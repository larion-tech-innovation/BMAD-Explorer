This skill applies Amazon's Working Backwards methodology to stress-test any product concept before committing to a PRD. Mary (Business Analyst) guides you through five structured stages — from concept clarity through press release, customer skeptic FAQ, internal stakeholder FAQ, and final verdict — producing a distillate ready for immediate PRD creation.

five-stages 5-Stage Process core workflow

Each stage builds on the last. The document file is updated after each stage so work is never lost. Progress is tracked in frontmatter for resumable sessions.

Stage 1 — Ignition Concept clarity + parallel research
ElementDetail
4 Essentials Customer (specific persona, not "users") · Problem (concrete, not vague) · Stakes (consequences if unsolved) · Solution concept (rough is fine)
Fast-track If user opens with a concept that clearly addresses all 4 essentials → skip extended discovery, proceed directly to Stage 2
Graceful redirect After 2-3 exchanges still unable to articulate a clear concept → agent offers: "You may benefit from running bmad-brainstorming first. Want me to redirect?"
Parallel subagents Artifact Analyzer — scans planning_artifacts/ and user-provided docs for relevant context
Web Researcher — covers market landscape, competitors, analogous products, market data
Pushback rule Agent does not accept vague answers — pushes back on "small businesses" (too broad), "saves time" (not a concrete problem), "AI-powered" (not a solution concept)
Stage 2 — Press Release Draft → self-challenge → invite → deepen
SectionContent expected
Headline Single-sentence product name + core benefit. No jargon. A non-expert reader understands it immediately.
Subheadline Expands headline: who it's for + what it does differently.
Opening paragraph City/date dateline. Describes the product launch and key customer impact in 2-3 sentences.
Problem The specific pain the product solves. Concrete. Stakes included. No exaggeration.
Solution How the product solves the problem. What it does, not how it works internally.
Leader quote Attributed to a named, titled person at the company. Explains why we built this — authentic, not marketing copy.
How It Works 3-5 concrete steps a customer takes. Specific enough that the reader can picture themselves doing it.
Customer quote Attributed to a specific type of customer persona. Describes the real impact on their life or work.
Getting Started URL, first action, pricing signal (if any). Closes the loop: what does the reader do next?
Quality tests Mom test — would a non-technical parent understand this? "So what?" test — does every sentence earn its place? Weasel word scan — flags "significantly", "revolutionary", "best-in-class", "leading", "world-class"
Stage 3 — Customer FAQ Agent plays customer skeptic · 6-10 questions · 5 angles
AngleWhat gets surfacedExample question
Skepticism Why this over existing alternatives? What makes it genuinely different? "We already use [X tool] for this — why would we switch?"
Trust Data handling, company viability, who is behind this, track record "What happens to my data if the company shuts down?"
Practical concerns Cost, setup time, integrations, migration effort, support model "How long does it take to set up and what do I need from my IT team?"
Edge cases Uncommon but real scenarios — what breaks at the edges? "What happens when I have [unusual data format / special permission model / offline scenario]?"
The hard question The question the team hopes nobody asks — the weakest point of the offering "Why should I trust a product that's only been on the market for 3 months with our production data?"
Answer protocol 2-4 lines per answer · Direct and concrete · Quantify where possible · Include escape hatch if the answer is incomplete ("We don't support X yet — our roadmap targets Q3") —
Stage 4 — Internal FAQ + Pre-mortem Agent plays stakeholder panel · 6-10 questions · 6 angles
AngleWhat gets surfaced
Feasibility What is the hardest technical problem? What unknowns remain? What do we need to prove before we can build confidently?
Business viability Unit economics (calibrated to concept type), competitive moat, sustainable differentiation, pricing model validity
Resource reality Do we have the team? The timeline? What are the unavoidable trade-offs if resources are constrained?
Risk What kills this? Worst-case scenario? What are the assumptions that, if wrong, invalidate the entire approach?
Strategic fit Why us? Why now? Why not in 12 months? What happens if a larger player launches this first?
The avoided question The question the founder or PM avoids bringing up — usually about a known weakness, a competitor advantage, or a dependency they haven't solved

Pre-mortem Protocol (embedded in Stage 4)

  • Step 1Assume the product has failed in 12 months. This is not a hypothesis — it is the premise.
  • Step 2Tell the story of WHY it failed. A narrative, not a list. Forces specificity.
  • Step 3Extract the top 5 failure reasons from that narrative. Ranked by likelihood × severity.
  • Step 4Convert to a structured risk register — categorized across 5 dimensions.
Risk Register — 5 Dimensions Gary Klein pre-mortem · PRFAQ Stage 4
DimensionCovers
Market Demand assumptions that may not hold · timing risk · competitive response · market size miscalculation
Technical Feasibility gaps · scalability assumptions · vendor / API dependency · integration complexity underestimation
Regulatory Compliance requirements · data privacy jurisdiction · licensing constraints · sector-specific rules
Team Skill gaps · capacity constraints · motivation / alignment · key-person dependencies
Execution Scope creep · coordination failures · timeline compression · dependency sequencing errors
Stage 5 — Verdict Candid assessment · 3 categories · not a score
CategoryMeaningWhat follows
Forged in Steel Concept is clear, compelling, and defensible. Customer need is concrete. Internal risks are identified and have credible mitigations. Proceed to PRD creation. Distillate is the primary input.
Needs More Heat Promising but underdeveloped. Core insight is sound, but significant gaps remain in customer clarity, competitive differentiation, or internal viability. Address specific gaps identified in the verdict, then re-run selected stages or proceed with known constraints documented.
Cracks in the Foundation Fundamental risks or contradictions present that must be resolved before a PRD would be valuable. Building on this foundation risks wasted sprint cycles. Stop. Address root-cause gaps — may require going back to brainstorming or pivoting the concept significantly.

distillate Distillate Output

After Stage 5, prfaq-{name}-distillate.md is produced automatically. Dense bullet points by theme — designed for LLM consumption in the next downstream step.

Distillate Structure prfaq-{name}-distillate.md
ThemeWhat gets captured
Rejected framings Concepts, headlines, or positioning angles considered and discarded during the PRFAQ — prevents re-litigating in the PRD
Requirements signals Concrete feature and constraint signals surfaced through FAQ answers — directly feeds PRD functional requirements
Technical constraints Feasibility boundaries, integration requirements, and architectural assumptions identified in Stage 4
Competitive intel Competitors identified by Web Researcher + positioning gaps + differentiation angles that held up to scrutiny
Open questions Unresolved questions that Stage 3 or 4 exposed but could not answer — must be addressed in PRD or discovery phase
Scope signals What is explicitly in-scope vs. deferred — derived from Stage 3 edge case handling and Stage 4 resource reality
Resource estimates Rough effort signals from Stage 4 feasibility and resource angles — directional, not binding
Verdict findings Stage 5 verdict category + key supporting evidence + specific conditions that must be met before PRD creation
  • The distillate replaces the Product Brief as the primary input to the Create PRD step
  • Narrative structure (full PRFAQ) is preserved in prfaq-{name}.md for human reading
  • Distillate is optimized for token efficiency — no prose, no context, only signal

§ WHY Why This Approach?

Question Without PRFAQ With PRFAQ Challenge
Why Working Backwards? Teams start with technology or features, then search for customers to fit what was built Start with the customer experience — work backwards to what must be built. Forces customer-first clarity before any implementation investment
Why press release format? Teams describe the product in vague, internal language that sounds good but commits to nothing Writing for "launch day" forces concrete success definition — vague products cannot produce clear press releases. The PR surfaces gaps before PRD writing begins
Why both Customer FAQ AND Internal FAQ? Teams address external objections but skip internal blind spots — fatal flaws discovered in Sprint 3 Customer FAQ surfaces external objections. Internal FAQ surfaces internal blind spots. Both are required: teams that skip Internal FAQ regularly discover fatal flaws after development has started
Why 6-10 questions per FAQ? 5 questions = too soft (misses the hard ones). 15 questions = unfocused (dilutes the critical ones) 6-10 hits the confidence/concreteness sweet spot — enough to cover all critical angles without producing noise that buries the important findings
Why distillate instead of the full doc? PRD authors receive a long narrative document and must extract the relevant signals themselves — slow and lossy PRDs need dense signal, not narrative structure. The distillate is token-efficient and optimized for LLM consumption in the next step — preserving all signal with none of the prose overhead

skills 3 · Skills That Help This Session

Facilitator Mindset bmad-prfaq · session quality
SkillWhy it matters
Persona specificity Stage 1 asks who is being hurt by what problem. The agent pushes back on vague personas. Prepare a named, grounded persona before the session starts.
Comfortable with honest gaps Customer FAQ (Stage 3) and Internal FAQ (Stage 4) probe the weakest points. "We don't know yet" is a valid answer — it becomes an explicit gap, not a hidden assumption. Defensive answers get flagged.
Strategic ambiguity tolerance Internal FAQ forces commitment to decisions like "why us, why now." Committing to "we will decide X before launch" is acceptable. Pretending X doesn't need to be decided is not.
Key differentiator clarity Stage 2 (Press Release) asks what makes this worth building versus the status quo. Know this before the session — the agent challenges its own draft and will push on differentiators that don't hold up.

per-step 4 · Per-Stage Preparation

Each stage has a specific readiness moment. Having these ready prevents pauses that break the Working Backwards flow.

Stage-Level Readiness bmad-prfaq · 5-stage process
StageWhat to have ready
Stage 1 · Ignition The specific problem statement in terms of a real person's daily pain — not a technology or solution framing. "The accountant spends 3 hours every Friday reconciling X" beats "there's a data automation problem".
Stage 2 · Press Release A sense of the key differentiator — the one thing that makes this worth building versus the status quo. The agent challenges its own draft; you need a real answer when it asks "why can't a competitor copy this in 6 months?"
Stage 3 · Customer FAQ Prepare honest answers to the hardest customer questions: failure modes, scope of claims, edge cases, trust concerns. "We don't know yet" is valid — it becomes an explicit gap. Vague answers are flagged as evidence of a gap.
Stage 4 · Internal FAQ Be ready to address: hardest technical problem, unit economics (or build vs buy for internal tools), resource reality, and "why us, why now." The agent plays skeptical stakeholder — not an ally.
Stage 5 · Verdict No preparation needed — the agent synthesizes. After the verdict, the distillate is auto-produced. Plan to use prfaq-{name}-distillate.md as the primary input to Create PRD.

Working Backwards — Amazon PR/FAQ Methodology
PrincipleDetail
Core premise Write the press release for the finished product before building anything. If you cannot write a compelling press release, the concept is not clear enough to build.
Stress-test from both sides Customer FAQ forces answers to the hardest questions users will ask. Internal FAQ forces feasibility, ROI, and strategic risk questions. Both must be answered before committing to development.
Competitive research built in The skill automatically spawns subagents to research the competitive landscape between Stage 1 and Stage 2. Training data ages fast — live web research is always run.
Explicit verdict The final stage delivers a structured judgment: Forged in Steel (solid), Needs More Heat (promising but gaps remain), or Cracks in the Foundation (fundamental risks must be resolved before PRD).
Press Release Quality Tests
TestWhat It Checks
Mom test Would a non-technical parent understand what this product does, who it's for, and why it matters? If not, the headline and opening need rewriting.
"So what?" test Every sentence must earn its place. Any sentence that does not directly serve customer understanding or credibility is cut or rewritten.
Weasel word scan Flags and removes: "significantly", "revolutionary", "world-class", "best-in-class", "leading", "powerful", "robust", "seamless", "cutting-edge", "innovative".
Jargon scan Any term requiring industry knowledge is either explained in plain language or replaced with a simpler equivalent.
Gary Klein's Pre-mortem — 4-Step Flow
StepWhat Happens
Premise It is 12 months from now. The product has failed. This is not a possibility — it is the assumed outcome for this exercise. Prospective hindsight surfaces failure modes that forward-looking planning misses.
Narrative Write the story of why it failed. Present tense. Specific. "In month 3, the key integration partner changed their API pricing, making our unit economics negative. The team couldn't pivot fast enough."
Extraction From the narrative, extract the top 5 failure reasons. Ranked by likelihood × severity. These are the real risks — not the risks that sound sophisticated, but the ones the narrative exposed as plausible.
Risk register Convert to structured register across 5 dimensions: Market · Technical · Regulatory · Team · Execution. Each entry: risk description, likelihood signal, impact signal, mitigation or monitoring approach.
