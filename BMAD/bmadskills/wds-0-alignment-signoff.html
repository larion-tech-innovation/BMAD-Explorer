This skill secures formal alignment between all stakeholders before any design or development begins. It is for the moment when multiple parties — client and supplier, manager and employee, board and team — need a documented agreement on scope, approach, and commitment. Saga runs a collaborative discovery conversation, synthesizes what she hears into a structured "pitch", and then optionally hardens that pitch into a formal contract or an internal signoff document.

Use it when a consultant or agency is proposing a solution to a client, when an internal team is seeking budget or executive approval, or in any scenario where misaligned expectations would later prove costly. It is the WDS entry point before the Project Brief — establishing that everyone has agreed to proceed, on the same terms.

§ WHY Why Alignment & Signoff? — The reasoning behind each design choice

why-align-first Why Align Before Building?

Misaligned expectations are the costliest failure mode in any project. When a client and a supplier — or a board and a team — believe they agreed to different things, the gap surfaces only after work is done, when changing course is expensive and trust is already strained. Securing alignment up front, while nothing has been built yet, is the cheapest possible moment to discover and resolve a disagreement. WDS puts this phase before the Project Brief precisely so that the people footing the bill and the people doing the work confirm they mean the same thing before a single decision compounds.

why-written-pitch Why a Written Pitch Document?

A verbal agreement lives only in the memories of whoever was in the room — and memories diverge. A written pitch becomes a shared reference every party can return to, and it survives turnover: when a stakeholder leaves or a new team member joins, the document still says what was agreed. Writing it also forces the implicit to become explicit. The act of stating the solution, the value, and the commitment in plain words exposes assumptions that would otherwise stay hidden until they caused a problem.

why-framing Why the Cost-of-Inaction + Commitment Framing?

Most proposals describe what will be built. Fewer make the case for why not acting is itself a decision with a price. Section 9, Cost of Inaction, names that price — it reframes the choice from "spend or save" to "act or absorb the risk", which is usually what actually moves a stakeholder to commit. Section 10, Our Commitment, closes the loop: it states the accountabilities and guarantees the supplier or team is willing to stand behind. Together they turn a pitch from a description into a mutual agreement — each side knows what it is getting and what it is owed.

why-optional Why This Phase Is Optional

Phase 0 exists for the specific situation where separate parties need to agree before work starts. When there are no such parties — a solo founder building their own product, or a team that already has a green light — there is nothing to align and no one to sign off, so the phase adds ceremony without value. WDS makes it optional so that the pipeline starts at the Project Brief by default, and the alignment phase is reached for only when documented agreement between stakeholders genuinely matters.

Phase A Start & Understand · 01a–01e 5 steps

01a · Understand Situation

Saga opens by asking the user to clarify their role and situation before any content is generated — are they a consultant proposing to a client, a business owner hiring suppliers, a manager seeking internal approval, or a solo builder who may not need alignment at all? This initial assessment ensures every subsequent step is routed correctly. The user must answer before the workflow proceeds.

01b · Determine If Alignment Is Needed

Based on the user's stated situation, Saga determines whether the alignment and signoff workflow is genuinely needed or whether the user should skip directly to the Project Brief. Users with full autonomy and no separate stakeholders are offered an early exit. Those who need alignment receive a brief overview of what the process produces and how it will work.

01c · Offer to Extract from Existing Documents

Saga offers the user an optional shortcut: paste or upload existing email threads, chat logs, briefs, or RFPs, and Saga will extract the relevant context rather than starting from scratch. The step is optional — users without existing materials skip directly to detecting the starting point. No pressure is applied to produce documents.

01d · Extract Information from Documents

If the user provides existing communications or documents, Saga extracts and maps the relevant details to the alignment sections — realizations mentioned, requirements discussed, concerns raised, timeline hints, and budget signals. Extracted content is summarised and confirmed with the user before being carried into the exploration phase. Verbatim copying and sensitive or irrelevant details are explicitly excluded.

01e · Detect Starting Point

Saga asks where the user naturally wants to begin: with a problem they've noticed (a realization-first approach) or with a solution they already have in mind (a solution-first approach). The answer determines which exploration step is loaded first — the workflow is flexible and follows the user's lead rather than forcing a fixed linear order.

Phase B Explore Sections · 02a–02k 11 steps

02a · Explore The Realization

Saga helps the user articulate what they have realized needs attention — the trigger that caused this project to exist. The agent asks probing questions to surface the underlying observation, then helps the user identify both soft evidence (testimonials, complaints, patterns) and hard evidence (metrics, analytics, test results) that support the realization. The goal is a 2–3 sentence statement backed by at least one piece of evidence.

02b · Explore The Solution

For users who start with a solution idea rather than a problem, Saga captures the solution first and then works backwards to uncover the underlying realization it addresses. The agent connects the solution to the problem, explores why it matters, and briefly examines other possible approaches — setting up the recommended-solution section that comes later without critiquing the user's idea prematurely.

02c · Explore Why It Matters

Saga guides the user to articulate the strategic importance of the project — who is affected, what they are trying to accomplish (jobs), what pain points the project relieves, and what gains it creates. The questions focus on specific people and groups rather than abstract value, keeping the section brief and human-centred.

02d · Explore How We See It Working

Saga draws out the user's high-level vision for how the solution operates — the general approach and core concept, not detailed specifications or technical requirements. The questions are deliberately flexible and work for software, processes, services, products, and strategies alike. The output is a brief, accessible overview that stakeholders without technical backgrounds can understand.

02e · Explore Paths We Explored

Saga asks the user to articulate alternative approaches they considered before settling on the recommendation. Showing that other paths were evaluated demonstrates thorough thinking to stakeholders and makes the recommendation more credible. If the user only has one path, that is explicitly fine — the agent accepts a single-path answer rather than fabricating alternatives.

02f · Explore Recommended Solution

Saga asks the user which approach they prefer and, crucially, why they prefer it over the alternatives explored in the previous step. The reasoning is captured alongside the recommendation itself. The agent does not choose the solution for the user — the user owns the recommendation, and the agent's role is to help articulate the case for it clearly.

02g · Explore The Path Forward

Saga explores the practical work approach — which WDS phases are likely needed, how the work will flow (research to design to handoff, or abbreviated), what level of design detail is required, and how the work will be handed off for implementation. The output is a high-level plan, not a detailed project schedule.

02h · Explore Value We Will Create

Saga shifts to a positive, forward-looking frame: what happens if the project succeeds? The user articulates their ambition, the expected outcomes, and the specific metrics that will indicate success. The agent encourages a confident "we're committed to this" tone paired with measurable success criteria — enthusiastic and scientific rather than hedged or vague.

02i · Explore Cost of Inaction

Saga frames the opposite question: what happens if the project does not proceed? The user explores the financial cost, opportunity cost, competitive risk, and operational impact of doing nothing. This section makes the case that inaction is itself a decision with a price — the most persuasive framing for stakeholders who are weighing whether to commit.

02j · Explore Our Commitment

Saga helps the user articulate the resources, time, budget, and team being committed to make the project happen, along with the potential risks or challenges that should be acknowledged upfront. Rough estimates are explicitly accepted — the agent does not pressure for precision the user does not yet have. Risks are treated as honest acknowledgements, not disqualifying weaknesses.

02k · Explore Summary

After all ten sections have been explored, Saga helps the user distil the key takeaways into a brief summary — the main points stakeholders should walk away remembering. The summary is deliberately concise and leaves readers to draw their own conclusion rather than over-interpreting on their behalf. This closes Phase B before the synthesis phase begins.

Phase C Synthesize & Present · 03a, 03b, 03d 3 steps

03a · Reflect Back

Before writing a single word of the document, Saga reflects back everything captured across all ten sections and asks the user to confirm accuracy. Each section is summarised in plain language, and the user is invited to correct, adjust, or clarify anything that does not match their intent. This reflection is a mandatory quality gate — Saga will not proceed to synthesis until the user confirms the reflected understanding is correct.

03b · Synthesize Document

With confirmed content in hand, Saga crystallises the conversation into the alignment pitch document — a clear, compelling narrative readable in 2–3 minutes, structured across all ten sections. The agent applies framework thinking to ensure the document flows logically (realization → why it matters → solution → value → cost of inaction → commitment) and saves the result to docs/wds-1-project-brief/pitch.md. No content is added that was not confirmed in the reflection step.

03d · Present for Approval

Saga presents the completed pitch document and walks the user through the stakeholder review process: share the document, gather feedback, iterate until everyone is on board, and only then move to the signoff step. The agent explicitly supports negotiation and revision — the alignment phase is collaborative. Signoff is not offered until the user confirms the pitch has been accepted by all parties.

Phase D Generate Signoff · 04a, 04b 2 steps

04a · Offer Signoff Document

Once the alignment pitch is accepted, Saga presents the three signoff formats: a Project Contract (for consultants whose client approved the pitch), a Service Agreement (for founders or owners whose suppliers approved), and an internal Project Signoff Document (for internal teams seeking budget or executive approval). A skip option is also available. The user's choice routes the workflow either to Phase E (external contract) or Phase F (internal signoff).

04b · Determine Business Model

Before building any contract section, Saga determines the payment structure — Fixed-Price, Hourly/Time-Based, Retainer, or Hybrid. Each option is presented with a clear description, example pricing, and an explanation of what it means for the contract structure. The selected model shapes every subsequent contract section, particularly scope boundaries, payment terms, and the not-to-exceed clause.

Phase E Build Contract · 05a–05l 12 steps

05a · Contract Section 1 — Project Overview

Saga pulls the realization and recommended solution from the accepted alignment pitch and builds the contract's Project Overview section. This establishes what the project is about and why it exists, setting shared context at the top of the legal document. Content is drawn directly from the pitch — nothing is invented.

05b · Contract Section 2 — Business Model

Saga formalises the business model selected in step 04b into contract language, specifying the payment model name and its key terms. For fixed-price projects this means the total price and what it includes; for hourly work, the rate and billing frequency; for retainers, the monthly amount, minimum hours, and overage rate. This section is the financial foundation for all subsequent contract sections.

05c · Contract Section 3 — Scope of Work

Saga builds explicit IN-scope and OUT-of-scope definitions to prevent disputes later. The path forward and deliverables list are pulled from the alignment pitch, and the user is then asked directly what work is included and what would require a separate change order. Scope boundaries are adapted to the business model — fixed-price contracts demand the sharpest boundaries.

05d · Contract Section 4 — Payment Terms

Saga builds the payment schedule and terms tailored to the selected business model. For fixed-price contracts, the agent presents payment-split options (50/50, 100% upfront, milestone-based) and explains why upfront payment is fair when the supplier is bearing cost-overrun risk. For hourly and retainer models, billing frequency, invoice format, and due dates are gathered from the user.

05e · Contract Section 5 — Timeline

Saga defines the project timeline by extracting milestone and delivery date information from the alignment pitch and filling in any gaps with the user. Key milestones, delivery dates, and critical deadlines are documented. At the end of this step, the workflow branches: retainer contracts continue to the Availability section (05f); all other models skip ahead to Confidentiality (05g).

05f · Contract Section 6 — Availability (Retainer Only)

For retainer contracts only, Saga builds an Availability section defining when the contractor is reachable, typical response times, meeting availability, and how urgent requests are handled. This section sets realistic expectations so retainer clients know when work will be done and when responses can be expected. It is skipped entirely for fixed-price and hourly models.

05g · Contract Section 7 — Confidentiality

Saga builds a mutual confidentiality clause protecting sensitive information shared by both parties during the project. The user chooses the confidentiality level (standard mutual, limited, or one-way) and the duration, and Saga explains the exceptions that are never confidential by law. The mutual framing is emphasised — both parties are protected equally, which builds trust rather than creating an adversarial dynamic.

05h · Contract Section 8 — Not-to-Exceed (Conditional)

Saga adds a not-to-exceed clause based on the business model: required for fixed-price contracts (where it equals the total project price), optional for hourly engagements (where the user may choose to cap total spend), and not applicable for retainer contracts (which already have a monthly cap). The clause is paired with a change-order process so both parties have a clear path for authorising work beyond the cap.

05i · Contract Section 9 — Work Initiation

Saga specifies exactly when work may begin — upon contract signing, on a specific date, after the initial payment is received, after written authorisation, or after particular conditions are met. The user selects from these options. This clause protects both parties: the contractor is not doing unpaid work, and the client is not being charged before they have given the go-ahead.

05j · Contract Section 10 — Terms and Conditions

Saga builds the legal framework covering how scope changes are handled (change orders), how the contract may be terminated by either party, who owns the intellectual property produced, how disputes will be resolved, and which jurisdiction's laws govern the agreement. The user is asked to specify governing jurisdiction and the contract language — both are required, not optional.

05k · Contract Section 11 — Approval

Saga creates the formal signature block for both parties — client and contractor (or owner and service provider, depending on the document type selected in Phase D). Party names and roles are gathered from the user rather than assumed, and date fields are included alongside each signature line to make the document legally binding upon signing.

05l · Finalize Contract

Saga reviews the complete contract with the user section by section, asks for any final adjustments, and confirms readiness before signing. The document is saved to docs/wds-1-project-brief/contract.md (or service-agreement.md). Once signed, alignment is achieved, commitment is secured, and the workflow is ready to proceed to the Phase 1 Project Brief.

Phase F Internal Signoff · 06a, 06b 2 steps

06a · Build Internal Signoff Document

For internal company projects that do not require an external contract, Saga builds a structured approval document covering project goals and success metrics, budget and resource allocation, ownership and decision-making authority, the approval workflow and required sign-off stages, and the project timeline with key milestones. Optional sections cover risks, confidentiality, and a high-level path forward. If the company has an existing signoff template, Saga offers to adapt the document to match it.

06b · Finalize Signoff Document

Saga presents the completed internal signoff document, explains the approval workflow (share with stakeholders, collect required signatures, follow the company's process), and asks for any final adjustments before the document enters the approval chain. Once approved, internal alignment is achieved, budget and resources are committed, and the project is ready to proceed to the Phase 1 Project Brief workflow.
