§ WHAT Brainstorming — What does this Skill do?

Skill code
bmad-brainstorming
Menu code
BP
Role
Business Analyst — Guided ideation facilitator. Mary leads structured brainstorming sessions across a library of 108 techniques (13 categories) to surface 100+ ideas from any problem space, in one of 3 selectable stances.
Input
Problem statement, current system context, rough scope — provided by the user at session start. Can be refined before ideation begins.
Output
_bmad-output/brainstorming/brainstorming-session-{YYYY-MM-DD-HH-MM}.md with YAML frontmatter containing stepsCompleted, session metadata, and full idea index — plus an append-only .memlog.md tagging each entry's author.

This skill transforms a rough problem statement into a structured idea library through guided, technique-driven ideation. It targets 100+ ideas per session, enforces anti-bias pivots every ~10 ideas to prevent anchoring, clusters ideas into natural themes, and produces a prioritized report that feeds directly into bmad-product-brief, bmad-prfaq, or bmad-create-prd.

3 stances Facilitator · Creative Partner · Ideate-for-me

Before technique selection, Mary asks which stance to take. The stance governs how much of the ideation work Mary does versus draws out of the user — it does not change which techniques are available.

Default
Facilitator
Mary asks, the user generates. Techniques structure the questions; ideas come from the user. Closest to the original guided-ideation behavior.
user does the generating
Collaborative
Creative Partner
Mary contributes ideas alongside the user — a genuine back-and-forth. Each idea's .memlog.md entry is tagged --by user or --by coach so provenance stays visible.
shared authorship, tagged
Delegated
Ideate-for-me
Mary generates the full batch from the stated problem and goal, with minimal back-and-forth. Best when the user wants a starting library to react to, not to co-create.
fastest · lowest user effort

memlog Append-Only Memlog & Authorship

Every idea is written once and never edited in place — the session's .memlog.md is an append-only log, and each entry carries who produced it.

  • --by userTags an idea the user originated — the default in Facilitator stance and for user contributions in Creative Partner stance.
  • --by coachTags an idea Mary originated — the default in Ideate-for-me stance and for Mary's contributions in Creative Partner stance.
  • Why it mattersProvenance stays visible through clustering and prioritization, so a stakeholder can later tell which ideas were the team's and which were AI-suggested.
  • Never rewrittenCorrections or refinements are appended as new entries, not edits to the original line — the memlog is the audit trail, brainstorming-session-{date}.md is the polished report built from it.

§ WHY Why Brainstorm Before PRD?

Writing a PRD from a first idea codifies the first idea. Brainstorming first generates the space of possible ideas — then the PRD captures the best of them, not the most obvious ones.

Dimension Without Brainstorming With Brainstorming First
Idea quality First 5–10 ideas are predictable, already well-explored by competitors Ideas 50–100 are the "golden zone" — non-obvious, underexplored, defensible
Cognitive bias Anchoring on the first framing; confirmation bias validates initial assumptions Anti-bias pivots every ~10 ideas systematically break anchoring and availability heuristics
Solution space Narrow — bounded by the initial problem statement as stated Wide — 100+ ideas reveal reframings of the original problem itself
PRD quality PRD reflects the team's prior knowledge, not the possible solution space PRD is built from a prioritized cluster of 100+ ideas with explicit Impact/Feasibility scoring
Team alignment Team aligns on the first idea proposed by the loudest voice Team aligns on a shared idea library — everyone contributed, clusters are visible

Why 100+ ideas? Research on creative productivity consistently shows that the first 20 ideas in any brainstorming session are the "obvious" ideas — things the group already knew. Ideas 20–50 begin to challenge assumptions. Ideas 50–100 are where genuinely novel, underexplored territory emerges. The 100+ target is not arbitrary; it is the threshold at which generative thinking shifts from retrieval to genuine creation.

anti-bias Why Anti-Bias Pivots?

Three cognitive biases are particularly destructive in brainstorming sessions:

  • Anchoring BiasThe first idea stated becomes the implicit reference point for all subsequent ideas. Every idea is evaluated relative to the anchor, constraining the exploration space.
  • Availability HeuristicIdeas that are easy to recall (recent, vivid, discussed before) dominate the session. Novel ideas that are hard to articulate get discarded prematurely.
  • Functional FixednessObjects and systems are only seen in their conventional roles. Users are users. Servers serve. Revenue comes from subscription. Pivots force alternative framings of every element.

Anti-bias pivots are announced explicitly every ~10 ideas — they are not a background process. The announcement itself is part of the cognitive reset. Pivot methods rotate through: persona shifts ("think as a CFO / 10-year-old / adversarial hacker"), hat shifts (Six Thinking Hats rotation), and question inversions ("how do we make this catastrophically fail?").

clustering Why Clustering?

100+ ideas without structure is noise. Clustering converts the idea volume into a navigable signal. Miller's Law establishes that working memory holds 7 ± 2 chunks — clustering maps 100+ ideas into 5–9 meaningful themes, making the full idea library cognitively manageable for downstream prioritization and PRD writing.

  • AI proposes 5–9 initial clusters based on natural idea affinities.
  • User approves, renames, splits, or merges clusters.
  • Outlier ideas that do not fit any cluster go to the "parking lot" — they are preserved, not discarded.
  • Cluster names become candidate epic names or product area labels in the resulting PRD.
  • Each cluster carries its constituent ideas with novelty scores and anti-bias tags.

progressive Why Progressive Flow Specifically?

Unstructured brainstorming suffers from premature convergence — the group evaluates ideas before generation is complete, chilling creative risk-taking. Progressive Flow prevents this by enforcing a strict four-phase journey: divergence in Phase 1, analysis only in Phase 2, refinement in Phase 3, planning in Phase 4. Evaluation is structurally prohibited until Phase 2.

  • Phase 1 (Expansive Exploration) explicitly prohibits evaluation — quantity over quality.
  • Phase 2 (Pattern Recognition) introduces analytical lenses but does not generate new ideas.
  • Phase 3 (Idea Development) converges the idea space by developing the best candidates.
  • Phase 4 (Action Planning) translates ideas into versioned roadmap commitments.

This structured journey is particularly valuable for complex, multi-dimensional problems where a simple "generate, then evaluate" model produces shallow results.

skills 3 · Skills That Help This Session

Facilitator Mindset bmad-brainstorming · session quality
SkillWhy it matters
Openness without self-censorship The biggest session killer is editing yourself before speaking. The filter comes in Steps 4–5 (Cluster + Prioritize), not during generation. Every idea is valid until it's ranked.
Domain fluency The richer your domain knowledge, the more specific and actionable your responses to the agent's probing questions. Vague answers produce vague ideas.
Short, concrete answers Two sentences per idea response is enough. The agent builds on your seed — long explanations slow the session without improving idea quality.
Stamina for the long stretch Ideas 50–100 feel forced — that discomfort is exactly where the value is. Research shows the "golden zone" sits at ideas 50–100, after the brain has exhausted familiar patterns and is forced to reach for more distant associations.

per-step 4 · Per-Step Preparation

Within the session, each step has a specific readiness moment. Having these ready prevents mid-session pauses that break the creative flow.

Step-Level Readiness bmad-brainstorming · 6-step process
StepWhat to have ready
Step 1 · Setup A 1–2 sentence topic statement that already contains who the users are and what problem is being explored. This is the seed the agent uses to design the method menu and session arc.
Step 2 · Generate Concrete examples of specific pain points or real scenarios you've observed — not abstractions. "The accountant has to manually re-enter data three times" beats "there's a data entry problem".
Step 3 · Anti-bias pivot No preparation needed — agent announces and drives pivots. Your job: stay open and respond from the new perspective rather than reverting to the previous angle.
Step 4 · Cluster Be ready to recognize patterns by purpose / audience / mechanism — not surface-level wording. "These 4 ideas all reduce manual steps" is a valid cluster even if the ideas look different on the surface.
Step 5 · Prioritize Know which framework fits your situation: Impact/Effort 2×2 (open exploration, no fixed constraints) or MoSCoW (resources fixed, scope flexible). The agent will ask — having this answer ready saves a discussion round.
Step 6 · Save No preparation needed — agent auto-generates the report. Optionally: decide in advance whether to use bmad-advanced-elicitation for deep-dive on top ideas before saving.

Core Technique List — 61 of 108 (remaining 47 in brain-selector.html)

1 · Structured (7 techniques)
Structured · 01
SCAMPER Method
Systematically apply 7 lenses: Substitute · Combine · Adapt · Modify/Magnify · Put to other uses · Eliminate · Reverse/Rearrange. Works on any existing concept or product.
Core convergent refinement tool
Structured · 02
Six Thinking Hats
Evaluate ideas through 6 colored lenses: White (facts) · Yellow (optimism) · Black (caution) · Red (emotion) · Green (creativity) · Blue (process). Used in Progressive Flow Phase 2.
De Bono framework · P0/P1/P2 triage
Structured · 03
Mind Mapping
Start with a central concept, branch into sub-themes, then sub-sub-themes. Reveals hierarchical relationships and unexpected lateral connections between clusters.
Visual + structural exploration
Structured · 04
Resource Constraints
Ideate under artificially tight constraints: zero budget, single engineer, 48-hour deadline. Constraints force creative reframing and often surface the most elegant solutions.
Creative limitation catalyst
Structured · 05
Decision Tree Mapping
Map ideas as decision branches with implementation paths, dependencies, and outcomes. Used in Progressive Flow Phase 4 to produce V1/V1.5/V2/V3 versioned roadmap.
Roadmap · V1–V3 versioning
Structured · 06
Solution Matrix
Plot ideas across two axes (e.g., Impact vs Effort, User Value vs Tech Complexity) to create a 2×2 or 3×3 priority matrix. Reveals where high-value, low-cost ideas cluster.
Visual prioritization grid
Structured · 07
Trait Transfer
Select an unrelated object or domain, list its key traits, then transfer each trait to your problem space. The friction of forced translation produces genuinely novel ideas.
Forced analogy catalyst
2 · Creative (11 techniques)
Creative · 01
What If Scenarios
Pose radical hypotheticals: "What if this product was free?" / "What if we had 10× the users overnight?" / "What if we removed the core feature?" Breaks assumption-bound thinking.
Progressive Flow Phase 1 anchor
Creative · 02
Analogical Thinking
Draw deep structural analogies from another domain. Understand how Netflix handles discovery, then ask: how could our problem apply the same discovery logic?
Cross-domain structural mapping
Creative · 03
Reversal Inversion
Invert the goal: instead of "how do we improve retention?" ask "how do we guarantee users quit?" The reversed list, flipped back, often reveals overlooked high-impact levers.
Anti-goal reframing
Creative · 04
First Principles Thinking
Strip the problem to its fundamental truths. Ignore conventions, industry norms, and prior solutions. Rebuild from physical or logical first principles. Musk's battery example.
Assumption destruction method
Creative · 05
Forced Relationships
Randomly combine two unrelated concepts and force a meaningful connection. The difficulty of the combination is the point — the brain works harder to find non-obvious links.
Random concept collision
Creative · 06
Time Shifting
Explore the problem from three temporal positions: how was this solved 50 years ago? How is it solved today? How might it be solved 20 years from now? Surfaces evolutionary patterns.
Temporal perspective rotation
Creative · 07
Metaphor Mapping
Select a rich metaphor (the product as a garden, as a river, as a city) and fully explore implications. What does "planting seeds" mean? What is "runoff"? Metaphors unlock non-literal insight.
Extended metaphor exploration
Creative · 08
Cross-Pollination
Import entire solution patterns from an adjacent industry. How does aviation handle crew resource management? How does Formula 1 handle pit stops? Apply the pattern to your domain.
Industry pattern transplant
Creative · 09
Concept Blending
Take two distinct existing ideas and merge them into a third that inherits properties of both but is genuinely new. "Uber for X" is a shallow blend — this technique goes deeper.
Conceptual fusion method
Creative · 10
Reverse Brainstorming
Instead of solving the problem, brainstorm all the ways to cause it or make it worse. Invert the resulting list. Often the most direct route to solutions people have unconsciously avoided.
Inversion catalyst · pairs with Reversal
Creative · 11
Sensory Exploration
Describe the problem through each sense. What does good onboarding feel like? Sound like? Smell like? Cross-sensory description activates non-verbal cognitive pathways.
Synesthetic reframing
3 · Collaborative (5 techniques)
Collaborative · 01
Brainwriting 6-3-5
6 participants write 3 ideas in 5 minutes, then pass sheets around. Simulated in solo sessions: generate ideas in batches, then treat each batch as if passed from a different "contributor".
Serial idea handoff pattern
Collaborative · 02
Round Robin
Each "voice" contributes one idea per turn, no evaluation during generation. In AI-facilitated sessions, Mary role-plays multiple stakeholder voices contributing in sequence.
Equal-voice idea generation
Collaborative · 03
Yes And
Improv-derived: accept every idea and build on it with "Yes, and..." Eliminates the evaluation instinct during generation. Chains of "Yes And" often produce surprisingly viable ideas.
Improv-based idea building
Collaborative · 04
Gallery Walk
Display all generated ideas visually, then "walk" through them as if in a gallery. In text sessions, Mary presents all ideas in a visual list and facilitates commentary passes.
Spatial idea review simulation
Collaborative · 05
Nominal Group Technique
Silent individual generation followed by structured group sharing and ranking. In solo sessions: generate silently first (no meta-commentary), then present and evaluate separately.
Generate-then-evaluate discipline
4 · Deep (8 techniques)
Deep · 01
5 Whys
Ask "why" five times in sequence to reach the root cause of the problem. Ideas generated at depth 4–5 are typically the most structurally sound and hardest for competitors to copy.
Root cause to root innovation
Deep · 02
Jobs to Be Done
Identify the underlying "job" the user is hiring your product to do. Strip features, strip UI — what is the fundamental progress the user is trying to make? Ideas from this level are durable.
Christensen JTBD framework
Deep · 03
Iceberg Analysis
Map visible events → underlying patterns → structural causes → mental models. Most innovation addresses events (surface). Ideas from the mental-model level change paradigms.
Systems thinking iceberg model
Deep · 04
Stakeholder Empathy
Deeply inhabit each stakeholder persona — not just primary users but peripheral ones (the user's manager, the user's customer, the regulator). Surface ideas invisible from a single vantage point.
Peripheral stakeholder lens
Deep · 05
Assumption Mapping
List all assumptions embedded in the current problem definition. Invert each one. Ideas that emerge when you remove foundational assumptions are often the most disruptive.
Assumption inversion at scale
Deep · 06
Value Chain Analysis
Map every step from raw input to delivered value. Identify which steps are bottlenecks, which add no value, which could be collapsed or automated. Innovation often lives at ignored handoff points.
Porter value chain application
Deep · 07
Pre-Mortem
Imagine the project has failed catastrophically. Describe what went wrong in detail. The failure modes reveal hidden risks; the mitigations suggest ideas not visible from a success mindset.
Failure-mode ideation source
Deep · 08
Second-Order Thinking
Ask not just "what happens if we do X?" but "what happens as a result of that result?" Second-order consequences surface ideas that address systemic effects, not just immediate ones.
Consequence chain exploration
5 · Theatrical (6 techniques)
Theatrical · 01
Character Immersion
Fully inhabit a character — a villain, a child, a visionary founder — and ideate entirely from their worldview, vocabulary, and values. Shedding your own perspective unlocks otherwise inaccessible ideas.
Radical persona embodiment
Theatrical · 02
Role Storming
Assign each idea-generating pass to a specific role: CFO, user, hacker, regulator, 10-year-old. Each role voices only what that character would care about. Forces truly different cognitive modes.
Multi-role sequential ideation
Theatrical · 03
Worst Possible Idea
Deliberately generate the worst, most catastrophic, most absurd ideas possible. Paradoxically, the worst ideas are liberating — and often contain the seeds of breakthrough insights when inverted.
Deliberate badness technique
Theatrical · 04
Future Newspaper
Write the front page of a newspaper reporting on your product's success (or failure) 5 years from now. Headlines force concision; the act of writing for an audience surfaces hidden strategic intent.
Journalistic future projection
Theatrical · 05
Superhero Powers
Grant the product or team a specific superpower (teleportation, telepathy, omniscience). Ideate with that power fully in play. Then ask: what is the closest real-world approximation of that power?
Power fantasy → real analog
Theatrical · 06
Press Conference
Simulate a press conference announcing a breakthrough product. Journalists ask hostile questions. Preparing the answers forces articulation of value, differentiators, and risk mitigation.
Adversarial Q&A as ideation
6 · Wild (8 techniques)
Wild · 01
Moonshot Thinking
Set a 10× improvement target, not 10%. 10% thinking produces incremental optimizations; 10× thinking requires abandoning the current model entirely, which is where genuine breakthroughs live.
Google X · 10× mandate
Wild · 02
Extreme Scenarios
Push conditions to absurd extremes: what if we had 1 billion users? 1 user? Zero budget? Infinite budget? Constraint extremes reveal which design decisions are brittle and which are universal.
Boundary condition exploration
Wild · 03
Random Word Stimulus
Open a dictionary to a random page, pick a word, and force a creative connection to the problem. Randomness breaks associative ruts by introducing completely unrelated cognitive seeds.
Serendipitous association catalyst
Wild · 04
Absurdity Injection
Introduce a deliberately absurd constraint or feature (the app must work underwater; all interactions must be voice-only; pricing must be based on lunar phases). Absurdity forces creative problem-solving.
Constraint via deliberate absurdity
Wild · 05
Time Travel
Visit the problem as solved by people in 1920, 2050, and 3000. Each era has radically different materials, assumptions, and values. Time-traveling across eras reveals which constraints are real and which are artifacts of now.
Temporal constraint removal
Wild · 06
Size Extremes
Redesign the product if it were the size of an atom. Now redesign it if it were the size of a continent. Size extremes surface interface, distribution, maintenance, and scale assumptions invisible at normal size.
Scale constraint exploration
Wild · 07
Material Alchemy
Reimagine the product as if built from an entirely different material substrate: light, water, sound, human attention, trust, reputation. Each material brings its own physics and design constraints.
Substrate reimagination
Wild · 08
Universe Inversion
Flip all core assumptions simultaneously: users become providers, problems become features, costs become revenues, weaknesses become differentiators. Total inversion often reveals the complementary anti-product.
Total assumption inversion
7 · Introspective Delight (6 techniques)
Introspective · 01
Personal Narrative
Connect the problem to a formative personal story. Authentic personal narrative surfaces values, emotional drivers, and lived insights that purely analytical methods miss entirely.
Story as insight source
Introspective · 02
Values Alignment
List core values (yours, your team's, your users') and ideate solutions that embody each value fully. Values-aligned ideas have stronger resonance and require less external motivation to build.
Value-led ideation
Introspective · 03
Intuition Mining
Start from gut feeling: "I have a vague sense that the answer involves X." Explore that intuition exhaustively before applying analytical frameworks. Intuitions encode compressed pattern recognition.
Pre-analytical signal exploration
Introspective · 04
Peak Experience Recall
Recall a peak experience of the product working brilliantly (or imagine one). Describe every detail of that experience. Reverse-engineer the conditions that made it possible.
Reverse-engineering excellence
Introspective · 05
Dream State
Describe the problem as if recounting a dream — associative, symbolic, non-linear. Dream logic surfaces connections the waking analytical mind censors as irrational.
Associative non-linear ideation
Introspective · 06
Joyful Obsession
Identify what you would work on obsessively for free. What makes the problem intrinsically fascinating rather than instrumentally important? Obsession-aligned ideas sustain through execution hardship.
Intrinsic motivation alignment
8 · Biomimetic (3 techniques)
Biomimetic · 01
Natural Selection Simulation
Generate many idea variants rapidly, then apply "selection pressure" — which variants survive a specific constraint? Iterate. Evolution finds solutions through variation and selection, not design.
Evolutionary idea iteration
Biomimetic · 02
Ecosystem Thinking
Model the product as an ecosystem: producers, consumers, decomposers, niches, energy flows. Which niche is unoccupied? What role could you play that stabilizes the whole? Ecosystems are self-sustaining.
Ecological role identification
Biomimetic · 03
Swarm Intelligence
Design solutions using simple local rules that produce complex global behaviors — no central control. Ant colonies, murmurations, immune systems. What are the local rules that create the desired emergent system?
Emergent behavior design
9 · Quantum (3 techniques)
Quantum · 01
Superposition Ideation
Hold two contradictory ideas simultaneously without resolving the contradiction. Explore what a product would look like that is simultaneously free AND premium, simple AND powerful, local AND global.
Paradox tolerance as creative tool
Quantum · 02
Entanglement Mapping
Identify deeply correlated elements in the problem space — pairs that change together regardless of distance. Changing one entangled element cascades non-locally through the system.
Non-local correlation exploration
Quantum · 03
Uncertainty Principle
Accept that measuring one dimension of the problem precisely makes another dimension fundamentally uncertain. This is not a bug — design for productive uncertainty and adaptive systems that thrive in ambiguity.
Designed ambiguity tolerance

techniques Technique Descriptions used in this skill

Detailed descriptions of the methods and frameworks the agent applies during a session. Understanding these helps you get more out of each technique when the agent invokes it.

Progressive Flow — 4-Phase Techniques

Progressive Flow bmad-brainstorming · Method 4
PhaseTechniqueDescriptionRules
Phase 1
Expansive		 What If Scenarios Ask "What if..." to break assumptions and constraint patterns. Forces the mind into possibility-space rather than problem-space. No judgment. No limits. Wild ideas welcome. Every "what if" is valid during generation.
Phase 2
Analytical		 Six Thinking Hats Analyzes ideas through 6 lenses: White (factual data + evidence) · Yellow (value + benefits) · Black (risks + weaknesses) · Red (emotional resonance) · Green (creative potential) · Blue (process fit). Assigns priority tiers: P0 (immediate) · P1 (near-term) · P2 (future) · Research track. Apply all 6 lenses before tier assignment. A fatal Black Hat finding (security flaw, legal risk, technical impossibility) downgrades to Research track regardless of other scores.
Phase 3
Refinement		 SCAMPER Refines ideas through 7 angles: Substitute · Combine · Adapt · Modify · Put to other uses · Eliminate · Reverse. Each angle is a question: "What can be combined?", "What can be eliminated?", etc. Apply to P0 + P1 ideas from Phase 2, not the full idea set.
Phase 4
Action		 Decision Tree Mapping Maps top ideas to a versioned roadmap: V1 (core, launch-critical) · V1.5 (fast-follow, high impact, low complexity) · V2 (strategic expansion) · V3 (long-horizon) · Research track (validate before committing). Identifies dependencies between ideas. V1 must be standalone — no dependencies on V1.5 or V2 for V1 to ship.

Anti-Bias Pivot Methods

Pivot Techniques bmad-brainstorming · Step 3 · mandatory every ~10 ideas
MethodHow it worksBiases it counters
Domain rotation Shift cognitive domain in sequence: UX → Architecture → Workflow → Reliability → Integration → Business Model → Distribution → repeat. Agent announces the new domain explicitly before each next prompt. Functional fixedness. Availability bias (generating only from familiar domains).
Persona shift "Now think as a CFO" / "Think as a 10-year-old" / "Think as an adversarial hacker" / "Think as a regulator". Forces a perspective that would not arise from primary-user empathy alone. Empathy tunnel (ideas only from one user's perspective). Status quo bias.
Question inversion "How do we guarantee this feature fails catastrophically?" — generate failure modes actively, then invert each failure mode into a success criterion or safeguard feature idea. Optimism bias. Confirmation bias (only generating ideas that confirm the initial hypothesis).

Clustering Methods

Clustering bmad-brainstorming · Step 4
MethodDescriptionKey rule
Affinity grouping AI proposes 5–9 clusters by natural affinity (purpose / audience / mechanism). User approves, renames, splits, or merges. Outlier ideas go to a parking lot — preserved, not discarded. Cluster names become candidate epic or product-area labels in downstream PRD work. Miller's Law (7±2): optimal cluster count for human working-memory retention. Too few → ideas lost inside large clusters. Too many → prioritization becomes unwieldy.
Six Hats cluster
(Progressive Flow)		 Clustering and prioritization happen within Phase 2 via six-lens analysis. Ideas strong on White + Yellow + Green Hats without a fatal Black Hat finding → P0. Strong value but execution risk → P1. Speculative or long-horizon → Research track. P0 must survive all 6 hats, not just 3.

Prioritization Frameworks

Prioritization bmad-brainstorming · Step 5
FrameworkWhen to useHow it works
4-Criteria scoring Default for Methods 1, 2, 3. Open exploration — no fixed resource constraints. Score each cluster on: Impact (user/business value) · Feasibility (technical realism) · Innovation (genuine novelty) · Alignment (fit with goal + strategy). Quick wins (high Impact + low Effort) flagged separately regardless of overall score.
Impact / Effort 2×2 When effort estimation is possible. Maps to 4 quadrants. Quick wins (high impact, low effort — do first) · Big bets (high impact, high effort — plan carefully) · Fill-ins (low impact, low effort — if bandwidth allows) · Thankless tasks (low impact, high effort — avoid, document as explicitly out).
MoSCoW Resources fixed, scope flexible — time-boxed sprints or fixed-budget features. Must (launch fails without this) · Should (high value, drop only if necessary) · Could (include if time allows) · Won't (explicitly out of this version — documented, not just deprioritized). The Won't list is as important as Must.
Decision Tree Mapping
(Progressive Flow)		 Progressive Flow Phase 4 only. Uses SCAMPER-refined idea set from Phase 3. Maps to versioned roadmap: V1 / V1.5 / V2 / V3 / Research track. Decision nodes identify dependencies (e.g., V1.5 feature requires V1 infrastructure). Research track items get explicit "what must be proven" statements.
