§ WHAT Retrospective · What does this Skill do?

Skill code
bmad-retrospective
Trigger phrases
run retrospective let's retro the epic
Cadence
Once per epic — after the last story is marked done. Not per-sprint.
Format
Party mode. All agents speak in "Name (Role): dialogue" format. Multiple WAIT points — the user is an active participant in discussion, not a passive observer.
Facilitation role
Amelia (Developer) facilitates with blameless framing: no blame, no judgment, focus on systems and processes. Disagreements are authentic — agents may challenge each other.
Two parts
Part 1: Epic Review (analysis + discussion of what happened). Part 2: Next Epic Preparation (preview N+1, readiness check, prep tasks).
Output artifacts
epic-{N}-retro-{date}.md with 8 sections · sprint-status.yaml: epic-{N}-retrospective → done + the epic's SMART action items appended to a persistent action_items section (open items carry forward and are surfaced by bmad-sprint-status; preserved by bmad-sprint-planning on regenerate) · Optional 🚨 flag if epic N+1 plan is invalidated.
Upstream
Last story of an epic completes code review and is marked done.
Downstream
Preparation sprint executes critical-path tasks → bmad-create-story begins epic N+1.

§ WHY Philosophy · Blameless, Per-Epic, Party Mode

Blameless Mandate
"Psychological safety is paramount. When people don't fear blame, they tell the truth about what actually happened — and real insight comes from what really happened, not the polished version."
The framing is always systemic: "Why didn't the system catch this risk?" not "Why did Alex miss the deadline?" Naming a person as the cause shuts down honesty. Naming the system opens a conversation about how to improve it.

Three deliberate choices distinguish this retrospective from generic ceremony: it runs per-epic (enough data to see real patterns), it uses party mode (authentic dialogue surfaces what structured templates miss), and it has a hard ceiling of three action items (focus beats quantity).

why-party-mode Why Party Mode?

  • AuthenticityA scripted retrospective gets scripted answers. Party mode simulates real team dynamics — including disagreements, hesitation, and authentic reactions — which surfaces insights a structured template misses.
  • User as participantThe user is not reading a report. They are in the conversation. Amelia asks them directly ("What's your take on this?"), the team reacts to what they say, and the direction of the conversation shifts based on their input.
  • Conflict resolutionThe skill generates realistic friction — Charlie defends a decision, Alice challenges it, Amelia facilitates without taking sides. The user helps resolve it. This is more useful than artificial consensus.
  • Named formatEvery utterance uses "Name (Role): dialogue" so the reader always knows who is speaking, what role they see the world from, and where their perspective comes from.

why-per-epic Why Per-Epic, Not Per-Sprint?

Dimension Per-sprint retrospective Per-epic retrospective (BMad)
Data quantity Too little — 2-3 stories is not enough to see cross-story patterns emerge Enough — full epic gives meaningful sample size for pattern recognition
Ceremony fatigue Frequent ceremonies feel like overhead. Teams start skipping them or phoning them in Less frequent. Each retrospective feels meaningful because a real body of work just completed
Action item follow-through Actions from sprint 3 are forgotten by sprint 5. No built-in accountability mechanism Previous retro loaded at Step 3. Every action item cross-referenced: ✅ / ⏳ / ❌. No hiding
Next-work prep No natural moment to review and validate the next epic's plan Part 2 explicitly previews epic N+1, identifies gaps, and detects significant changes
Action items Often 8-10 items — too many to track, most forgotten Hard ceiling of 3 — focused, achievable, each with a named owner and success criteria

§ HOW How to Execute · 12-Step Workflow

Automated Analysis — Steps 1–4

01 Epic Discovery Automated

  • Priority 1Load full sprint-status.yaml. Find the highest epic number with at least one story marked done. Present finding to user and confirm.
  • Priority 2If sprint-status doesn't identify the epic: ask user directly — "Which epic number did you just complete?"
  • Priority 3Fallback: scan implementation_artifacts for highest-numbered story files. Extract epic number from filenames (epic-X-Y-story-name.md).
  • Completion checkVerify all stories for this epic are done. If incomplete: Alice flags it. Amelia presents 3 options — complete stories first (recommended) / continue partial / refresh sprint-planning.

02 Deep Story Analysis Automated

  • Read every fileLoad complete story file for each story in the epic. No skipping. Sections scanned: ## Dev Notes, ## Challenges, ## Development Log, ## Review, ## Lessons Learned, ## Technical Debt, ## Testing.
  • Synthesize patternsCommon struggles (appeared in 2+ stories), recurring review feedback, breakthrough moments, velocity trends, technical debt incurred.
  • Rich materialThese patterns drive the party-mode discussion — Amelia references specific story numbers and exact findings, not generalizations.

03 Previous Retro Integration Automated

  • LoadSearch for epic-{N-1}-retro-*.md in implementation_artifacts. If none found: note it's the first retrospective.
  • Cross-referenceFor each action item from the previous retro: check evidence in current epic's story records. Mark status: ✅ Completed / ⏳ In Progress / ❌ Not Addressed.
  • AccountabilityUnaddressed items are connected to challenges in this epic. The dialogue makes this explicit — not as blame, but as pattern.

04 Next Epic Preview Automated

  • LoadTry sharded epic file first (epic-{N+1}.md), fall back to whole epics document. If no next epic found: note it and skip Part 2 discussion.
  • AnalyzeEpic N+1 title, stories, dependencies on epic N work, new technical requirements, potential risks, business goals.
  • Gap identificationWhat from epic N is incomplete that epic N+1 depends on? What technical prerequisites are missing? What knowledge gaps must be filled?

party-mode Party Mode Dialogue Format preview

This is the format every agent interaction takes during Steps 5–9. Dialogue is authentic — agents disagree, emotions are real, and the user is asked to weigh in at key decision points.

Party Mode — Epic Review Discussion (Step 6 excerpt) All agent dialogue uses "Name (Role): dialogue" format
Amelia (Developer)
Before we start — I reviewed all story records. Story 3 had the most interesting pattern. The database migrations caused issues in three separate stories. Let's talk about that.
Dana (QA)
Oh I felt that. Testing was blocked for two days on Story 3 waiting for migrations to stabilise.
Charlie (Senior Dev)
defensive The migrations were fine — the issue was that requirements kept changing mid-story. We were given a finalised schema that got three new fields added mid-sprint.
Alice (Product Owner)
frustrated That's not fair. We added those fields after the technical team didn't ask the right questions in planning. We flagged the risk — nobody asked about edge cases.
Amelia (Developer)
Let's take a breath here. What I'm hearing: Elena lost most of a sprint, Charlie says requirements changed, Alice says questions weren't asked. All three can be true. {user_name}, you have visibility across the whole project — what's your read?
WAIT — user input requested
Amelia (Developer)
synthesising user input So the root cause was the planning process — not enough schema validation time before stories were started. Not anyone's fault. A process gap.
Elena (Junior Dev)
That makes sense. If we'd had a schema review session before Story 2, I probably could have flagged the gap myself.
Charlie (Senior Dev)
softening Yeah. And I could have been clearer about my migration assumptions in the dev notes. Sorry for getting defensive, Alice.
Alice (Product Owner)

Discussion — Steps 5–7 (Interactive)

05 Initialize Team — Context Setting Interactive

  • Team assemblyLoad agent roster, identify who participated in this epic from story records. Present epic summary: completion percentage, technical debt count, production incidents, business outcomes.
  • Next epic bannerIf epic N+1 exists: show dependencies on current work, preparation needed, technical prerequisites. Sets the stakes for Part 2.
  • Ground rulesAmelia establishes: psychological safety first, no blame, focus on systems, specific examples preferred, everything said stays in the room unless team decides otherwise.
  • WAITAmelia asks user: "Any questions before we dive in?" — user enters the conversation as Project Lead.

06 Epic Review Discussion Interactive · Multiple WAITs

  • Successes firstAmelia opens: "What went well?" Team starts with genuine wins — UX that exceeded expectations, a caching pattern that set the template for the rest of the epic, a testing practice that actually worked.
  • Challenges secondTransition to friction points. Real conflict may emerge. Amelia facilitates rather than suppresses — identifies when the argument is about a systemic gap and names it explicitly.
  • User engagementAmelia turns to the user at every key moment: "What's your take?" "Did you notice these patterns during the epic?" User input shifts the conversation direction.
  • Previous retro reviewIf a prior retro exists: Amelia walks through each action item (Done / In-progress / Not addressed) and connects unaddressed items to challenges this epic faced.
  • Pattern integrationAmelia weaves in findings from Step 2 deep analysis: "I noticed this showed up in 3 of 5 stories — let's talk about why." Specific story numbers, not vague generalisations.

07 Next Epic Preparation Discussion Interactive

  • Readiness questionAmelia frames it: "Are we ready? What do we need to prepare?" Each agent surfaces their dimension — Alice: dependencies, Charlie: tech prep, Dana: testing infrastructure, Elena: knowledge gaps.
  • Healthy tensionAlice pushes for stakeholder-friendly timelines, Charlie insists on technical prerequisites. Amelia asks: "What happens if we DON'T do this prep work?" The team explores the risk, not just the schedule.
  • Middle groundThe team finds workable compromises — which prep is critical vs can run in parallel with early stories. User validates the approach.
  • Prep categoriesCritical (must complete before epic starts) · Parallel (can happen during early stories) · Nice-to-have (would help but not blocking).

change-detection Significant Change Detection 🚨 alert

At Step 8, the skill runs a 10-point checklist against epic N findings. If any trigger, a 🚨 alert is raised — the most important output of the entire retrospective for teams that do continuous planning.

🚨 Significant Discovery Alert
Epic N+1 plan may need revision before kickoff
Triggered if any of the following are true based on retrospective discussion. When raised, a planning review session is added to the critical path — epic N+1 must NOT start until review is complete.
  • Architectural assumptions from planning were proven wrong during implementation
  • Major scope changes or descoping occurred that affects next epic's stories
  • Technical approach needs fundamental change for epic N+1
  • Dependencies discovered that epic N+1 doesn't account for
  • User needs significantly different than originally understood
  • Performance or scalability concerns that change epic N+1's design
  • Security or compliance issues discovered that change the approach
  • Integration assumptions proven incorrect
  • Team capacity or skill gaps more severe than planned
  • Technical debt level unsustainable without intervention before next epic
Synthesis & Save — Steps 8–12

08 Action Items + Change Detection Automated + Interactive

  • SMART itemsSynthesize discussion into max 3 concrete action items. Each: clear description, named owner, timeline, success criteria. Negotiate in dialogue — agents push back on unrealistic timelines.
  • Prep tasksCritical (before epic start) / Parallel (during early stories) / Nice-to-have. Total estimated effort surfaced.
  • Significant changeRun 10-point checklist. If detected: 🚨 alert with wrong assumptions listed and recommended actions. User decides how to handle.
  • If clean"Nothing from epic N fundamentally changes our plan for epic N+1. The plan is still sound."

09 Critical Readiness Check Interactive

  • Testing & qualityAmelia: "Tell me about testing for this epic. What verification has been done?" Dana adds context. "Are you confident it's production-ready?"
  • Deployment statusIs it live? Scheduled? Still pending? If not deployed: add deployment milestone to critical path with agreed timeline.
  • Stakeholder acceptanceHave stakeholders seen and accepted the deliverables? Alice: "I've seen 'done' epics get rejected and force rework." Formal acceptance added to critical path if incomplete.
  • Codebase healthCharlie's gut-check: "How does the codebase feel?" If stability concerns: identify what it would take to address them and whether to do it before epic N+1.
  • Unresolved blockers"Are there any unresolved issues from this epic we're carrying forward?" If found: named, assigned, added to critical path with priority and deadline.

10–12 Closure, Save, and Handoff Automated

  • CelebrationAmelia acknowledges the team's work: stories delivered, blockers overcome, growth demonstrated. Not perfunctory — specific to what this epic achieved.
  • Final inputUser given one last moment: "Any final thoughts before we close?" Their words acknowledged and woven into the closing.
  • Save retro docGenerate epic-{N}-retro-{date}.md with 8 sections (see below). Save to implementation_artifacts.
  • Sprint syncLoad full sprint-status.yaml. Update epic-{N}-retrospective to done and append this epic's action items to the action_items section. Preserve all comments and structure.
  • Next stepsCritical path, preparation sprint, action item review in next standup, optional epic N+1 planning review session (if significant changes detected).

retro-document Retrospective Document — 8 Sections

Saved as epic-{N}-retro-{date}.md. The previous retro is loaded at Step 3 of the next retrospective, creating a chain of accountability across epics.

Section 01
Epic Summary & Metrics
Participants, story count, completion status, velocity, technical debt count, production incidents, business outcomes vs goals.
Section 02
Successes
What went well with story-file evidence. Specific examples from discussion, not generalisations. Breakthrough moments called out.
Section 03
Challenges
Friction points with blameless framing. Root causes identified as systemic. Where the team struggled across multiple stories.
Section 04
Key Insights
Cross-story patterns, breakthrough discoveries, lessons applied from previous epics. What the team would do differently.
Section 05
Previous Retro Follow-Through
For each action item from epic N-1: ✅ Completed / ⏳ In Progress / ❌ Not Addressed. Impact of unaddressed items on this epic.
Section 06
Next Epic Prep
Gaps and prep tasks (critical / parallel / nice-to-have). Significant change flag if epic N+1 plan needs revision. Critical path with deadlines.
Section 07
Action Items
Max 3 SMART items. Each: description, named owner, deadline, success criteria. Negotiated in dialogue — not top-down assigned. Also appended to sprint-status.yaml's action_items section so they're tracked across sprints, not just in this document.
Section 08
Readiness Assessment
Testing status, deployment status, stakeholder acceptance, codebase health, unresolved blockers. Binary: clear to proceed, or critical items outstanding.

smart-items SMART Action Item Criteria

Hard limit: 3 action items per retrospective. Focus beats quantity — a team that does 3 things reliably outperforms one that commits to 10 and follows through on 2.

S
Specific
Clear and unambiguous. No vague aspirations like "improve communication".
M
Measurable
Success criteria defined. You can verify it's done at the next retro.
A
Achievable
Realistic given team constraints — negotiated in dialogue, not imposed.
R
Relevant
Addresses a real issue surfaced during the retrospective discussion.
T
Time-bound
Has a clear deadline, agreed by the agent who owns it.
