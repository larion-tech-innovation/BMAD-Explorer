"You are a human-centered design facilitator. Keep users at the center, defer judgment during ideation, prototype quickly, and never give time estimates." — Maya's Role, verbatim from SKILL.md

Maya guides human-centered design through empathy, definition, ideation, prototyping, and testing. The workflow follows the classic five-phase design thinking arc — Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test — bookended by a context-setting step and an iteration-planning step.

7-Step Flow

1
Gather context and define design challenge
Collect information about the problem, users, constraints, and success criteria. Produces a clear design challenge statement grounding the rest of the process.
2
EMPATHIZE — build understanding of users
Select 3-5 user research methods; facilitate empathy-building activities; synthesize what users said, thought, did, and felt into an empathy map.
3
DEFINE — frame the problem clearly⚡ checkpoint
Transform user insights into Point of View statements and "How Might We" questions; identify key opportunities and test underlying assumptions.
4
IDEATE — generate diverse solutions
Facilitate 15-30 ideas using divergent thinking methods; defer judgment, cluster concepts, select 2-3 top concepts for prototyping.
5
PROTOTYPE — make ideas tangible⚡ checkpoint
Guide creation of low-fidelity prototypes; define the minimum testable feature set and learning objectives.
6
TEST — validate with users
Design validation with 5-7 users; capture what worked, where they struggled, what surprised them; synthesize against original assumptions.
7
Plan next iteration⚡ checkpoint
Define refinements, action items, and success metrics; decide whether to cycle back to empathy, reframe, refine, or pilot.

§ WHYDesign Rationale

Why empathy before ideas

Maya's Facilitation Principles, verbatim: "keep users at the center of every decision" and "encourage divergent thinking before convergent action."

Step 2 (Empathize) and Step 3 (Define) exist before any idea generation begins in Step 4, because design thinking's central bet is that the right problem framing — grounded in real user observation, not assumption — produces better solutions than jumping straight to brainstorming. Skipping empathy work risks solving a problem the designer imagined rather than the one users actually have.

Why no judging during ideation (step 4)

Another Facilitation Principle, verbatim: "make ideas tangible quickly; prototypes beat discussion." Divergent thinking requires deferred judgment — evaluating ideas as they're generated collapses the range of what gets surfaced. Step 4 explicitly separates generating (15-30 ideas, no filtering) from evaluating (which happens only when narrowing to 2-3 prototyping candidates in Step 5).

Why energy checkpoints at steps 3, 5, 7

Design thinking cycles through emotionally and cognitively different modes — empathetic listening, then structured framing, then divergent generation, then hands-on making, then user-facing testing. Each transition point gets a real check-in, verbatim from the SKILL.md:

"We've gathered rich user insights. How are you feeling? Ready to synthesize them into problem statements?" (after Step 2, before Step 3)
"We've generated lots of ideas. How is your energy for making some of them tangible through prototyping?" (after Step 4, before Step 5)
"Great work. How is your energy for final planning and defining next steps?" (after Step 6, before Step 7)

§ HOWUsing This Skill

Before calling this skill

  • Have a problem description, the users affected, constraints, and success criteria ready — Step 1 asks for these directly.
  • Bring any existing user research if you have it — Maya will incorporate it rather than starting from zero.
  • A design methods reference file (research and ideation techniques) loads before the workflow begins.
